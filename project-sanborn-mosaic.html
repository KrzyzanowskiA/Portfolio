Hollowing of Second Street: Land Use, Interstates, and Disinvestment in the Center of Black Capitalism

A look into how parcel use along Second Street within the Jackson Ward neighborhood changed over time.

  • RoleStudent Researcher
  • PartnersHJWA · Virginia Commonwealth University
  • TimelineSummer 2026 - Present
  • ToolsArcGIS Pro, HTML, R, Excel

The question

Jackson Wards Second Street, or the Deuce, was once known as “Black Wall Street” and “the Harlem of the South.” The streets layout shifted over time from being focused on financial institutions to entertainment. The construction of I-95 cut this street in half, causing a decline in business.

This project aims to look at the changes in land use along the street on a block by block level.

What I did

The project started by collecting old directory data from Second Street from 1880 to 2025. Parcels were then given a first and second level identification in order to categorize them for analyses. A transition matrix was then used to track how the parcels changed identification over time. Parcels were then looked at to see which blocks changed the most and which were most stable.

Then, dissimilarity and churn were calculated for each of the blocks over time. This allowed for us to see if one specific identification was more prevalent in a block and how much changed the block went through.

What I found

Second Street was mainly residential in the 1880s and over time became more commercial. 1950 marked the peak of commercial parcels along Second Street with nearly 40% of parcels having that identification. We also saw how blocks above i-95 were more resistant to change than those below I-95, being that they were frequently residential.
